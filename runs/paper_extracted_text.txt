 a b s t r a c t
To address the severe challenge posed by composite intermittent sampling repeater jamming (ISRJ) to aerospace 
radar, we propose a novel end-to-end deep learning framework for simultaneous signal separation and parameter 
estimation. At the core of this framework is a custom deep separation network (CISRJ-SN), which features a 
unique hybrid attention architecture. This architecture synergistically fuses one-dimensional convolution for 
local feature extraction with a gated attention unit for global dependency modeling, thereby achieving high-
ﬁdelity jammer signal separation even at a low Jammer-to-Noise Ratio (JNR). Results from both simulations and 
real-world hardware-in-the-loop experiments collectively validate the superior performance of our framework. 
In two-component and multi-component scenarios, it achieves accuracy rates of 99.5 % and 94.4 % respectively, 
marking a signiﬁcant improvement over the next-best methods. In addition to high accuracy, the framework 
demonstrates exceptional estimation precision, reducing the Mean Absolute Error (MAE) of key parameters by 
over 60 %. This proves the high stability and reliability of its estimation results, oﬀering a promising solution for 
future intelligent sense-and-countermeasure closed-loop systems.
1.  Introduction
State-of-the-art radar systems constitute the core sensory compo-
nent of modern aerospace platforms-including airborne, spaceborne, 
and missile-borne systems-playing a pivotal role in navigation, guid-
ance, and battleﬁeld surveillance [1–4]. Integral to these systems, ad-
vanced antenna technology serves as the critical interface to the elec-
tromagnetic environment, directly inﬂuencing the system’s operational 
range, resolution, and its susceptibility to interference [5,6]. In fact, 
a class of anti-jamming techniques known as spatial ﬁltering is im-
plemented at the antenna level, employing methods such as adaptive 
null-steering and sidelobe cancellation to suppress interfering signals 
from speciﬁc directions [7,8]. However, such spatial-domain methods 
have inherent limitations when countering sophisticated interference 
that can mimic authentic target echoes and may enter through the 
main antenna lobe. Notably, a particularly potent form of this threat is 
the composite Intermittent Sampling Repeater Jamming (ISRJ), which 
leverages Digital Radio Frequency Memory (DRFM) technology [9].

Existing strategies to counter the threat of ISRJ generally fall into 
two primary categories [11,12]. The ﬁrst category involves proactive

istics, thereby impeding the jammer’s ability to eﬀectively follow and 
counter. Along similar lines, references [14,15] achieve interference 
suppression via the joint optimization of the transmit waveform and the 
receive ﬁlter. The primary merit of such approaches lies in their ability 
to mitigate jamming at its source. Their fundamental limitation, how-
ever, is that their eﬃcacy  is critically dependent on a priori knowledge 
of the jamming intent and modality. Consequently, in complex and dy-
namic electromagnetic environments characterized by unknown or un-
predictable jamming patterns, the robustness and adaptability of these 
strategies are severely constrained.

This has led to a second class of strategies garnering signiﬁcant re-
search attention: passive suppression techniques predicated on the pre-
cise estimation of jamming parameters. The precise estimation of the 
type, quantity, and critical parameters of composite jamming signals 
constitutes the essential a priori information required for subsequent 
interference cancellation, ﬁlter optimization, or the design of targeted 
countermeasures [16–19]. The research in this domain can be broadly 
demarcated into two principal phases of development:
1) The initial phase is characterized by approaches rooted in con-
ventional signal processing. In this stage, researchers sought to leverage 
artifact signatures in the pulse compression domain or employ time-
frequency analysis (TFA) tools to deinterleave and estimate the param-
eters of ISRJ signals [20,21]. The primary strengths of these approaches 
are their high degree of physical interpretability and comparatively low 
computational complexity. Their critical drawback, however, is an acute 
sensitivity to the jamming-to-noise ratio (JNR); in low-JNR scenarios, 
signiﬁcant signal features become inundated by noise, resulting in a 
precipitous decline in performance. Crucially, in scenarios involving 
composite jamming from multiple sources, the constituent signals of-
ten overlap in both the time and frequency domains, exhibiting severe 
parameter coupling. This renders eﬀective decoupling and precise esti-
mation intractable for conventional methods.
2) Subsequently, to surmount the limitations inherent in conven-
tional approaches under low-JNR and feature-coupling conditions, the 
research community shifted its focus toward solutions predicated on 
deep learning (DL) [22]. Early DL-based methods sought to enhance 
performance by integrating handcrafted, domain-speciﬁc features. For 
instance, reference [23] exploited the temporal periodicity inherent in 
dual-component ISRJ signals to conduct parameter estimation. Refer-
ence [24], in turn, introduced the concept of object detection to this do-
main, employing an AE-YOLOv8 model to draw bounding boxes around 
individual jamming components. The primary contribution of this line 
of inquiry was its validation of DL’s potential for automated feature ex-
traction. However, their limitations stemmed from this very reliance 
on speciﬁc, pre-deﬁned features: the periodicity exploited in [23] be-
comes obscured as the number of jamming components increases, while 
the bounding-box mechanism in [24] suﬀers from a signiﬁcant lack 
of robustness in low-JNR environments. Fundamentally, these methods 
failed to escape the conﬁnes of manual feature engineering, resulting in 
limited generalization capabilities.
In summary, to achieve genuinely intelligent processing, we propose 
a parameter estimation methodology predicated on signal separation 
networks. This approach learns the intrinsic representations of signals 
in a data-driven manner, thereby obviating the need for speciﬁc, hand-
crafted features. Tailored to the characteristics of composite ISRJ sig-
nals, we have constructed an end-to-end framework for simultaneous 
jamming separation and parameter estimation, introducing a novel sep-
aration network designated CISRJ-SN. This network leverages a state-
of-the-art encoder-decoder architecture. The core of this network is a 
novel hybrid Attention Block that deeply integrates a Gated Attention 
Unit (GAU) [25]-engineered for eﬃcient  long-sequence modeling-with 
one-dimensional convolutional networks. This fusion synergistically en-
hances the model’s capacity for both local feature characterization and 
global dependency modeling. Our holistic framework integrates jam-
ming separation, pulse detection, and clustering optimization to actu-
alize a direct mapping from raw mixed-signal input to ﬁnal parameter 
output, all within a monolithic model. It thereby provides a key techno-
logical underpinning for enhancing the situational awareness and self-
protection capabilities of aerospace platforms in future high-density, in-
tensely adversarial electromagnetic environments.
The innovations and contributions of this paper are summarized as 
follows:
1) We propose the Composite ISRJ Separation Network (CISRJ-SN), 
a novel interference separation model founded on a Gated Single-
Head Transformer (GSHT) architecture. By employing a convolution-
augmented self-attention mechanism, this model is the ﬁrst to learn 
both local feature patterns and global long-range dependencies 
within a uniﬁed, attention-gated framework. This breakthrough not 
only yields a substantial improvement in interference separation per-
formance but also provides novel perspectives and methodologies for 
addressing analogous problems.
2) To overcome the parameter estimation challenges posed by the tem-
poral fragmentation of separated signals, we introduce a robust al-
gorithm predicated on pulse detection and clustering optimization. 
This methodology integrates adaptive dynamic thresholding, multi-
stage pulse segmentation, and advanced clustering algorithms, en-
abling precise parameter estimation that is speciﬁcally tailored to 
the characteristics of the deinterleaved ISRJ signals.
3) The proposed methodology ﬁrstly demonstrates high eﬃcacy  not 
only in scenarios involving Linear Frequency Modulated (LFM) radar 
signals but also exhibits exceptional performance when processing 
Frequency-Hopping (FH) signals. This inherent adaptability and ﬂex-
ibility render it particularly well-suited for systems that employ di-
verse radar waveforms.
4) Our method demonstrates superior accuracy compared to state-of-
the-art techniques, particularly under low-JNR conditions. More-
over, even when trained on limited datasets, it markedly enhances 
estimation accuracy in resource-constrained scenarios, thereby of-
fering robust support for applications where data is scarce.
2.  Principles and signal models of ISRJ
This section outlines the fundamental operating principles of ISRJ 
and presents the signal model for its composite form.
2.1.  Mechanism and characteristics of ISRJ
ISRJ was initially proposed by Wang et al. in 2007 [26] and is im-
plemented via a transceiver architecture employing time-division mul-
tiplexing. The jammer operates by periodically sampling segments of 
the radar signal to generate a series of deceptive pulses that are highly 
phase-coherent with the authentic signal. Upon processing by the radar’s 
matched ﬁlter, each of these deceptive pulses can capture approximately 
50 % of the pulse compression energy, thereby producing a cluster of 
false targets in the range domain that exhibits a distinct amplitude gra-
dient. This composite jamming mechanism integrates the dual tactical 
eﬀects of deceptive target generation and suppressive noise-like blanket-
ing [27], signiﬁcantly enhancing the operational eﬀectiveness of elec-
tronic warfare (EW) systems.
Based on its intercept-and-forward strategy, ISRJ is primarily cat-
egorized into two main types: Direct Forwarding Jamming (DFJ) and 
Repetitive Forwarding Jamming (RFJ). The mechanisms of these two 
jamming types are analyzed in detail in the following subsections.
The operating principle of DFJ is illustrated in Fig. 1a. In this mode, 
the jammer’s receiver and transmitter operate in a time-division multi-
plexing manner. First, the jammer intercepts a segment of the incom-
ing radar signal during its ‘sampling’ phase (e.g., pulse segment ‘1’). It 
then immediately switches to the ‘forwarding’ phase to transmit the just-
recorded segment ‘1’. This ‘sample-and-forward’ process is then repeated 
in an alternating fashion-sampling ‘3’ and forwarding ‘3’, sampling ‘5’ 
and forwarding ‘5’. The resultant jamming signal can be mathematically 
expressed as:

3.  Composite ISRJ separation and parameter estimation
The composite ISRJ parameter estimation framework proposed in 
this paper adheres to a cascaded ‘separate-then-estimate’ design method-
ology, the overall workﬂow of which is depicted in Fig. 3. This frame-
work comprises two principal modules. The entire process begins at the 
input stage, where a composite ISRJ signal-a complex mixture of multi-
ple jamming components and noise-is fed into the system.
1) Stage One: Interference Separation (CISRJ-SN):
The raw signal ﬁrst enters the CISRJ-SN. This core module lever-
ages a unique hybrid attention mechanism, engineered to precisely 
separate the individual ISRJ components from a highly complex and 
interwoven input signal. The output of this stage is a set of sepa-
rated ISRJ component signals, where each ideally corresponds to an 
individual jammer, though it may still be fragmented and contain 
residual noise.
2) Stage Two: Parameter Estimation:
Subsequently, each separated ISRJ signal is independently pro-
cessed by a dedicated parameter estimation module. This module 
executes a suite of algorithms to accurately quantify the critical pa-
rameters for each jamming component. This process includes:
Adaptive Thresholding and Binarization: First, the fragmented sig-
nal is transformed into a stable binary sequence representing its 
‘on/oﬀ’ states.
Parameter Decoupling and Clustering Optimization: Raw measure-
ments for slice width and forwarding width are extracted from the 
binary sequence. A density-based clustering algorithm is then em-
ployed to reﬁne these measurements, removing outliers and identi-
fying the dominant values.
Final Parameter Output: Finally, the module computes and outputs 
the precise estimated values for each component: slice width ( ̂𝑇𝑙), 
forwarding width ( ̂𝑇𝑓 ), and the number of forwardings ( ̂𝑁𝐹 ).

3.1.2.  Masking net
The masking network constitutes the core of the separation frame-
work, tasked with the critical function of performing a non-linear map-
ping from the mixed feature representation generated by the encoder to 
𝐶 distinct masks, one for each jamming source. The overall workﬂow of 
this network is depicted in Fig. 4a.
1) Input Pre-processing: The feature sequence ℎ ∈ 𝑅𝑁×𝐿 from the en-
coder (where 𝐿 is the number of time steps and 𝑁 is the feature di-
mension) ﬁrst undergoes a pre-processing stage. It is passed through 
a Layer Normalization step to improve training stability, after which 
Positional Encodings are added to provide temporal order informa-
tion.
2) Core Separation Module: The pre-processed features are fed into 
a core separation module comprising multiple stacked Attention 
Blocks. These blocks iteratively reﬁne the features in a serial conﬁg-
uration. Each Attention Block, through the synergistic interplay of 
its internal convolutional operations and self-attention mechanism, 
performs hybrid modeling of both ﬁne-grained local features and 
global long-range dependencies. Furthermore, residual connections 
between these blocks ensure eﬀective gradient propagation, which 
enhances the stability of the deep network’s training process.
3) Mask Generation and Output: Following the deep processing by the 
ﬁnal Attention Block, the feature sequence enters the mask genera-
tion stage. It is ﬁrst passed through a ReLU activation function, after 
which a pointwise convolution expands the feature dimension from 
𝑁 to 𝑁 × 𝐶 to estimate the respective mask vectors for the 𝐶 tar-
get sources. Finally, a Sigmoid activation function normalizes these 
mask vectors to the range [0, 1]. Each value in this range can be intu-
itively interpreted as a conﬁdence score-representing the likelihood 
that the features at a given time step belong to a speciﬁc source-
thereby providing a precise weight for the subsequent multiplicative 
masking operation.

3.1.3.  Attention block
As illustrated in Fig. 4b, the Attention Block serves as the core engine 
that drives the separation process within the CISRJ-SN network. Moving 
beyond the monolithic architecture of the standard Transformer [29], 
we have engineered a hybrid architecture that deeply integrates 1-D 
Conv with a gated attention mechanism. The design draws its inspiration 
from the GAU [25], an architecture proposed for eﬃcient  long-sequence 
modeling. It is engineered to synergistically and eﬃciently  extract the 
ﬁne-grained local features within composite ISRJ signals while simulta-
neously modeling their global dependencies [30].
The core processing workﬂow of this module can be conceptualized 
as a parallel process of feature extraction and gated fusion:
1) Parallel Feature Generation
The input sequence, 𝑋 ∈ 𝑅𝐿×𝑁 , is concurrently passed through 
three independent 1-D Conv branches. This parallel processing yields 
three distinct tensors, 𝑈 , 𝑉 , and 𝐻, each designated for a speciﬁc 
function:
𝑈 = Conv1D(𝑋), 𝑉 = Conv1D(𝑋), 𝐻 = Conv1D(𝑋) (5)
where, each Conv1D operation denotes a 1-D Conv block. This pro-
cess generates three parallel feature pathways, each with a distinct 
function:
• Gating Path: This path generates the feature tensor 𝑈 ∈ 𝑅𝐿×2𝑁 , 
which functions as a data-driven ‘gate’ to dynamically control the 
subsequent ﬂow of information.
• Content Path: This path yields the feature tensor 𝑉 ∈ 𝑅𝐿×2𝑁 , 
which represents the ‘value’ of the signal. It contains the core in-
formation content that is subsequently weighted and selected by 
the attention mechanism.
• Attention Path: This path produces the feature tensor 𝐻 ∈ 𝑅𝐿×𝑁 , 
which is exclusively dedicated to the computation of attention 
weights. The ‘query(𝑄)’ and ‘key(𝐾)’ vectors for the attention 
mechanism are derived from this tensor.
For the U and V pathways, we employ an expansion factor of 2, 
increasing the feature dimension from 𝑁 to 2𝑁. This design is in-
spired by the Gated Linear Unit (GLU) [31] paradigm and serves 
a dual purpose: it enhances the network’s representational capac-
ity and, more importantly, facilitates a powerful data-driven gating 
mechanism. The expanded 𝑈 tensor acts as an intelligent ﬁlter, dy-
namically controlling the information ﬂow from the 𝑉  (content) ten-
sor. In contrast, the 𝐻 pathway (the attention pathway) maintains 
the dimension 𝑁. This is because its function is highly specialized: it 
is used solely to generate the 𝑄 and 𝐾 required for computing atten-
tion weights. Expanding the dimension for this pathway would not 
only unnecessarily increase the computational complexity of the at-
tention mechanism but would also be inconsistent with its core task 
as a basis for similarity computation. This asymmetric design en-
sures both clarity in the functional roles and overall computational 
eﬃciency  of the model.
2) Position-Aware Attention Computation
The feature tensor 𝐻, originating from the attention pathway, 
is ﬁrst enhanced through a Scale & Oﬀset adjustment and is then 
infused with positional information via Rotary Position Embed-
ding (RoPE) [32]. The convolution ﬁrst extracts local patterns with 
translation equivariance, after which RoPE endows these patterns 
with their absolute/relative temporal context. This enables the self-
attention mechanism to compute similarity based not only on fea-
ture content but also on their position within the sequence, thereby

3.2.2.  Parameter reﬁnement via clustering-Based optimization
By traversing the binary sequence generated in the previous step, we 
initially obtain two datasets containing numerous raw measurements: a 
set of slice widths (𝑊𝑙) and a set of forwarding widths (𝑊𝑓 ). However, 
the binarization process, no matter how eﬃcient,  is inevitably aﬀected 
by residual noise, which can lead to measurement jitter at the pulse 
edges or, in rare cases, generate spurious pulses caused by noise spikes. 
Consequently, these raw measurement datasets are unavoidably con-
taminated by a certain number of outliers. Directly averaging this noisy 
data would yield unreliable results. To mitigate the eﬀects of these bina-
rization errors, we employ a density-based clustering algorithm. This ap-
proach allows us to purify the true, dominant parameter values from the 
noisy statistical datasets. It works by identifying dense clusters of mea-
surements corresponding to the true parameter values while eﬀectively 
isolating and discarding the sparse outliers generated by binarization in-
accuracies. This method ensures that the ﬁnal parameter estimates are 
both accurate and resilient to noise.
We deﬁne a similarity metric: two data points, 𝑤𝑖 and 𝑤𝑗 , are consid-
ered similar if their absolute diﬀerence ∣ 𝑤𝑖 − 𝑤𝑗 ∣, is less than or equal 
to a predeﬁned threshold 𝜀 (e.g., 0.25μs). Otherwise, they are deemed 
dissimilar.
The setting of the similarity threshold 𝜖, should be matched with 
the system’s sampling interval 𝑇𝑠𝑎𝑚𝑝. A general guideline is to set 𝜖 as 
an integer multiple of the sampling interval, i.e., 𝜖 = 𝑁tolerance × 𝑇samp , 
where 𝑁𝑡𝑜𝑙𝑒𝑟𝑎𝑛𝑐𝑒 is an integer representing the tolerable number of sam-
pling points of jitter. In this study, the system sampling rate is 100 MHz 
(𝑇𝑠𝑎𝑚𝑝=0.01 𝜇𝑠), and we chose 𝑁𝑡𝑜𝑙𝑒𝑟𝑎𝑛𝑐𝑒 =25, thus setting 𝜖=0.25 𝜇𝑠. 
For systems employing diﬀerent sampling rates, this guideline can be 
used for adaptive adjustment, thereby ensuring the generalizability of 
the method.
To this end, our core strategy is to employ a density-based clustering 
algorithm. This approach allows us to purify the authentic, dominant pa-
rameter values from the noisy statistical dataset by eﬀectively isolating 
them from the outliers.

4.  Experimental analysis
The preceding sections have elaborated on the theoretical design of 
our proposed framework, but how does it perform in complex, real-
world environments? To systematically address this pivotal question, we 
have designed a series of rigorous and progressively challenging experi-
ments to provide robust empirical support. Our experimental evaluation 
is structured around the following key research questions:
1) Performance Superiority: Does our proposed method exhibit a sig-
niﬁcant advantage in measurement accuracy compared to current 
state-of-the-art techniques?
2) Environmental Robustness: Can the method maintain stable and re-
liable performance under adverse electromagnetic conditions, such 
as low JNR environments?
3) Model Eﬃciency  and Generalization: How dependent is the method 
on the volume of training data? Can it overcome the limitations of 
conventional methods, which are often restricted to LFM signals, and 
demonstrate equal eﬃcacy  for complex waveforms like frequency-
agile signals?
4) Practical Application Potential: Can the outstanding performance ob-
served in simulated environments be successfully translated to appli-
cation scenarios involving real-world hardware systems?
4.1.  Dataset
To ensure the simulation closely approximates practical scenarios, 
the parameters for each ISRJ component within the composite signal 
were randomly generated. The JNR was also varied randomly within a 
range of -10 to 20 dB. Using MATLAB, we generated datasets for both 
dual-interference and triple-interference scenarios. For each scenario, 
the dataset was partitioned into 5000 samples for training, 1000 for 
validation, and 1000 for testing. The speciﬁc parameter conﬁgurations 
are detailed in Table 1.
4.2.  Training setup
The model was trained on a single NVIDIA GeForce RTX 4060 GPU 
with 8 GB of VRAM. For optimization, we utilized the scale-invariant 
signal-to-distortion ratio (SI-SDR) [33] as the training loss function, 
a metric widely adopted for evaluating source separation tasks. The 
Adam optimizer [34] was employed to train the model for a total of 
100 epochs, with a learning rate of 1𝑒−3 and a batch size of 8. To ensure 
training stability, gradient clipping was applied to limit the norm of the 
gradients to 5. For regularization, we used weight decay with a coef-
ﬁcient of 1𝑒−4. Furthermore, we implemented a learning rate schedul-
ing strategy: the learning rate was multiplied by 0.5 if the validation 
loss did not improve for 3 consecutive epochs. Other network hyper-
parameters are shown in Table 2. The model framework and dataset 
will be made publicly available at the following GitHub repository:

4.4.3.  Evaluation of model eﬃciency  and generalization capability
This section evaluates the learning eﬃciency  and generalization ca-
pability of our proposed framework across diﬀerent signal modalities 
through two distinct sets of experiments.
1) Performance Analysis with Varying Training Sample Sizes
In authentic aerospace electronic warfare environments, acquiring 
and annotating vast, diverse datasets of jamming signals is often pro-
hibitively diﬃcult  and expensive. Consequently, a model capable of ef-
ﬁcient learning from limited samples to achieve high performance is of 
immense practical value. To this end, we benchmarked our proposed 
method against the AE-YOLOv8 and CSNet-ISRJ baselines under identi-
cal conditions. Each model was trained on incrementally larger training 
sets, with sample sizes progressively increasing from 1000 to 5,000, and 
their performance was subsequently evaluated on a ﬁxed test set of 1000 
samples.
As illustrated in Fig. 6, the performance of all models improves with 
an increasing number of training samples. However, at every data-scale 
increment, our method consistently and signiﬁcantly surpasses the com-
parative methods in measurement accuracy. The most critical ﬁnding 
is that our approach, when trained with only 2000 samples, achieves 
performance comparable to, or even exceeding that of, the CSNet-ISRJ 
model trained on the full 5000-sample dataset.
2) Adaptability Validation for Diverse Waveforms
To enhance their anti-jamming capabilities, modern radar systems 
often employ complex waveforms, such as those with frequency agility. 
A practical interference parameter estimation system, therefore, must 
not be conﬁned to analyzing conventional LFM signals but must be ca-
pable of adapting to the radar’s own complex signal schemes. To vali-
date the generalization capability of our method, we replaced the stan-
dard LFM signals in our dataset with frequency-agile signals and re-
evaluated our method’s parameter estimation performance under both 
dual-component and multi-component interference scenarios.
As presented in Table 5, our method demonstrates exceptional per-
formance when processing frequency-agile signals, achieving total ac-
curacies of 97.6 % and 83.4 % in dual- and multi-component scenar-
ios, respectively. This superior generalization capability stems from the 
fact that our time-domain model learns the structural patterns of the 
interference rather than its speciﬁc morphology in the time-frequency 
domain. Consequently, when the signal’s internal modulation changes 
from LFM to frequency-agile, methods reliant on time-frequency
morphology (such as AE-YOLOv8) fail due to drastic changes in 
the target features. In contrast, our approach maintains its perfor-
mance stability by capturing the fundamental behavioral nature of the
interference.

4.4.4.  Experimental validation with measured data
To deﬁnitively validate the eﬀectiveness and reliability of our pro-
posed framework in a real-world physical environment, we constructed 
a Hardware-in-the-Loop (HIL) experimental platform. As depicted in 
Fig. 7, this platform comprises a multi-channel interference simulator, a 
target generator, and a radar system. To ensure comparability with the 
preceding simulations, the core radar and interference parameters were 
kept consistent with those used in the simulation experiments. Due to 
the power limitations of the hardware system, we generated four distinct 
power levels of jamming signals by adjusting the transmission attenua-
tion. A total of 2000 measured data samples were acquired by collect-
ing 500 samples for each conﬁguration. This dataset was subsequently 
partitioned into training, validation, and test sets according to a 6:2:2
ratio.
Analysis of the acquired data reveals the inherent complexity of real-
world signals. As shown in Fig. 9, the measured composite jamming 
signals exhibit signiﬁcant time-frequency overlap, consistent with our 
simulations. More interestingly, we observed a ‘Forwarding Idle’ phe-
nomenon (Fig. 8) that was not present in the idealized simulations. This 
phenomenon refers to unexpected signal dropouts within certain peri-
ods, even when the jammer is conﬁgured with an integer-multiple duty 
cycle. These non-ideal eﬀects are likely caused by hardware latency or 
imprecise timing control.
The measured data was subsequently input into our model, with the 
ﬁnal parameter estimation results summarized in Table 6. As can be seen 
from the table, our method continues to perform exceptionally well, 
even when confronted with measured data containing non-ideal eﬀects. 
It achieved total accuracies of 97.4 % and 93.5 % in the dual- and multi-
component scenarios, respectively. These results are highly consistent 
with the performance achieved in our simulation experiments (99.5 % 
and 94.4 %, respectively), demonstrating only a marginal diﬀerence in 
performance.

To validate the eﬀectiveness and robustness of our proposed 
framework from two perspectives-internal architecture and external 
parameters-we designed the following experiments.
4.5.1.  Ablation of core architecture components
To validate the eﬃcacy  of the individual innovative components 
within our proposed CISRJ-SN framework, we designed a series of ab-
lation studies.
1) Validating the Necessity of the Attention Mechanism: To demon-
strate the indispensable role of the attention mechanism in captur-
ing global dependencies, we designed a ‘convolution-only’ variant. 
In this version, the GAU component within our ‘Attention Block’ was 
removed, leaving only the deeply stacked 1-D Conv structure. This 
eﬀectively degrades the model into a purely convolutional, deeper 
network.
2) Validating the Contribution of the Convolutional Module: To ver-
ify the contribution of the convolutional module in capturing local 
features, we designed a ‘quasi-pure attention’ variant. Speciﬁcally, 
the deeply integrated 1-D Conv component within the ‘Attention 
Block’ was either signiﬁcantly weakened or replaced with a simple 
linear layer, making the architecture more closely resemble a stan-
dard Transformer encoder layer.
As presented in the Table 7, the comparative experimental results 
demonstrate the superiority of our redesigned hybrid attention masking 
network. Secondly, the performance degradation observed upon remov-
ing either the attention mechanism or the convolutional fusion quantita-
tively validates the necessity of our synergistic ’convolution-attention’ 
design. Finally, the loss in accuracy incurred when using the simpli-
ﬁed parameter estimation algorithm underscores the value of our robust 
post-processing methodology.

5.  Conclusion
Composite Interrupted Sampling Repeater Jamming poses a critical 
threat to aerospace radar platforms. To mitigate this challenge, we pro-
pose an end-to-end parameter estimation framework built upon a novel 
hybrid attention architecture. Both simulations and hardware-in-the-
loop experiments conﬁrm that the proposed framework achieves supe-
rior performance under challenging conditions.
Quantitatively, our method achieves an overall measurement accu-
racy of 94.4 % in complex multi-component scenarios, representing a 
10.3 % relative improvement over the state-of-the-art CSNet-ISRJ ap-
proach. Beyond accuracy, the framework delivers outstanding precision, 
reducing the mean absolute error (MAE) of sampling interval estimation 
by more than 60 %, indicating far greater stability and reliability. More-
over, its robustness has been validated in low JNR environments: even 
when JNR drops to -5 dB, the measurement accuracy of key parameters 
remains above 90 %. Its remarkable data eﬃciency  and adaptability to 
diverse waveforms, such as frequency-agile signals, further highlight its 
potential for enhancing the electromagnetic self-protection capabilities 
of aerospace platforms.
Despite these promising results, we acknowledge certain limitations. 
Although the training dataset is diverse, it does not cover all conceivable 
ISRJ variants. In addition, the pursuit of high performance comes with 
a moderate increase in model complexity. Future work will focus on ex-
panding the dataset to include more sophisticated jamming strategies, as 
well as applying model compression and data augmentation techniques 
to improve real-time performance and generalization.

Appendix A.  Mathematical Principles of the Synergy between 
Convolution and RoPE
In our hybrid Attention Block, the one-dimensional convolution (1-D 
Conv) and Rotary Position Embedding (RoPE) are not simply stacked; 
instead, they form a close synergistic and complementary relationship in 
terms of functionality. The core idea is that the convolution is responsi-
ble for constructing local, structured features with translation equivari-
ance, while RoPE encodes the positional relationships of these features 
within the global sequence, thereby enabling the attention mechanism 
to perceive long-range dependencies. Below, we will mathematically 
elucidate their respective roles and derive how they work in synergy.
